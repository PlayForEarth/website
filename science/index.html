Behavioral Science

Awareness doesn't change behavior.
Infrastructure does.

Fifty years of behavioral research point to the same conclusion: the most reliable way to change what people do is to change the system they operate in — not to change their minds. Here is what the evidence says, and how Play for Earth is built around it.

01
The Default Effect

People follow the path
of least resistance.

When a system is designed so that participation is the default — and opting out requires deliberate effort — participation rates are dramatically higher. This isn't about convincing anyone. It's about which action is easier.

Opt-out vs opt-in
Johnson & Goldstein, 2003 — Science

Organ donation rates across European countries varied from under 20% to over 90% — not because of culture or attitudes, but solely because of whether the default was opt-in or opt-out. The behavior was the same. The system design was different.

90%+
Opt-out systems
~15%
Opt-in systems
Savings enrollment
Madrian & Shea, 2001 — Quarterly Journal of Economics

When employees were automatically enrolled in 401(k) savings plans, participation jumped from 49% to 86% — without changing incentives, salaries, or education. The default was the intervention. Most participants never changed the default contribution rate, either.

49%
Opt-in enrollment
86%
Auto-enrolled

"Small changes in the choice architecture — the way options are presented — can have large effects on behavior without restricting freedom of choice."

Thaler & Sunstein, Nudge (2008)
⚙️
What this means for circular programs

Recovery programs that require retailers or reps to opt in, sign up, or take extra steps will always underperform. The program needs to be built into the existing routine as the default path.

🔎
What Play for Earth does

We embed the collection point check into the existing sales rep visit. It's not an additional task — it's part of the route. The default action at the outlet now includes the circular verification step.

📊
The result

Compliance is no longer a function of motivation or awareness. It's a function of system design. Reps don't need to remember to check — not checking requires deliberate effort.

02
Visible Infrastructure

A visible system sends
a behavioral signal.

Recycling and recovery rates rise significantly when infrastructure is present and visible — not because people are more motivated, but because the infrastructure communicates: this is what people here do. Physical presence changes the perceived norm.

Recycling infrastructure
Schultz et al., 1995 — Journal of Environmental Psychology

Communities with visible, accessible recycling infrastructure participated at rates 3–4x higher than comparable communities without it — independent of awareness levels. Knowledge of why to recycle had almost no additional effect once bins were present.

3–4x
Higher participation with visible infrastructure
Deposit return systems
OECD — Deposit Refund Systems for Beverage Containers

Countries with structured deposit return systems consistently achieve recovery rates above 90%. The infrastructure — machines, containers, return points — makes returning the obvious and expected behavior. Enforcement plays a minor role.

98%
Germany
95%
Norway
92%
Lithuania

"The presence of a container signals that recycling is expected. Its absence signals the opposite — regardless of how strongly people are told to recycle."

U.S. National Academies of Sciences — Recycling Infrastructure Report, 2025
🛍
The basket is the signal

When a collection basket is present at the retail outlet, it communicates that circular behavior is expected here. Its absence communicates the opposite. The object itself is a behavioral intervention.

📸
Verification reinforces the signal

The act of photographing the collection point — basket in place, poster visible — strengthens the behavioral signal for both the rep and the retailer. The check-in makes the norm explicit and measurable.

🔄
Consistency compounds

Each verified visit reinforces the expectation. After several consistent check-ins, retailers begin to prepare before the rep arrives. The infrastructure has created an anticipatory behavior pattern.

03
Social Norm Theory

People do what they think
others are doing.

Descriptive norms — the behavior that appears normal in an environment — are among the most powerful drivers of human action. When a system is present and consistently used, it creates the appearance of a norm. Others conform to that norm without requiring explicit persuasion.

Descriptive norms
Cialdini, Reno & Kallgren, 1990 — Journal of Personality and Social Psychology

In a series of field experiments, littering dropped by over 50% when environments were kept clean and when visible evidence of others' behavior (clean floors, full recycling bins) was present. People inferred the norm from what they observed, not from what they were told.

>50%
Reduction in littering from norm cues alone
Hotel towel reuse
Goldstein, Cialdini & Griskevicius, 2008 — Journal of Consumer Research

Messages telling hotel guests that most guests in this room reused their towels outperformed standard environmental messages by 26%. The descriptive norm — this is what people here do — was more persuasive than any appeal to values or environment.

+26%
More reuse with descriptive norm message
👥
Network effects in the field

When retailers in a zone see neighboring shops with collection baskets, the behavior normalizes. The GTI Network Engagement score (15% of the index) captures exactly this dynamic — whether circular behavior is spreading across a network.

🏆
Leaderboards as norm signals

Territory-level dashboards and rep rankings make the descriptive norm explicit and competitive. Reps see what their peers are achieving. The benchmark becomes a social reference point that drives upward conformity.

📈
The GTI measures norm adoption

Moving from Stage 1 (Declared) to Stage 3 (Embedded) is precisely the transition from individual behavior under pressure to group norm adoption. The GTI tracks this progression quantitatively across territories.

04
Habit Formation

Incentives start behavior.
Repetition makes it permanent.

Habits form through repetition in stable contexts. The more consistently a behavior occurs in the same environment, the more automatic it becomes. Incentives are valuable for initiating behavior — but the goal is always to reach the point where incentives are no longer required.

Habit automaticity
Lally et al., 2010 — European Journal of Social Psychology

In a 12-week study of habit formation, behaviors performed consistently in stable contexts became automatic after an average of 66 days. Context stability was the single most important predictor — more than motivation, intention, or reward size.

66
Average days to automaticity
18–254
Range depending on complexity
Gamification & streaks
Hamari, Koivisto & Sarsa, 2014 — Hawaii International Conference on System Sciences

Gamification elements — points, badges, streaks, and leaderboards — significantly increased engagement and return behavior in longitudinal studies. Streaks in particular create loss aversion: users avoid breaking them even when the original incentive becomes less relevant.

+40%
Return behavior with streak mechanics

"Habits are not formed by motivation. They are formed by repetition in a consistent context. Once formed, they persist with minimal cognitive effort."

Wood & Rünger, 2016 — Annual Review of Psychology
🎪
Lucky Spin as habit trigger

The gamification layer in Play for Earth — points, streaks, and the Lucky Spin — is designed to sustain behavior through the 66-day window until automaticity sets in. After that, the incentive can be reduced without behavior loss.

📅
The route is the stable context

Sales reps follow consistent routes to the same outlets on recurring schedules. This is exactly the stable context that habit science identifies as ideal for automaticity. The circular check-in happens in the same place, at the same time, every week.

🤝
67% retention at 90 days

Across active pilot cohorts, 67% of retailers remain active at 90 days without incentive reminders. This is the behavioral signature of habit formation — participation has become the expected pattern, not the exceptional event.
